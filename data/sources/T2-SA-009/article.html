On January 24, 2026, 37-year-old US citizen Alex Pretti was repeatedly and fatally shot by United States Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents in Minneapolis, Minnesota, United States. The incident was the third shooting by federal officers in three weeks, following the killing of RenÃ©e Good and a shooting of a Venezuelan man, and occurred amid mass protests against Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE).

Background

Main article: Operation Metro Surge
See also: Killing of RenÃ©e Good

The incident was the third shooting by ICE agents in Minneapolis in three weeks. On January 7, a federal agent fatally shot RenÃ©e Good, an incident that set off protests that drew thousands.[1] The following week, an immigration officer shot a Venezuelan man in the leg.[2]

Shooting

On January 24, 2026, at 9:05 a.m.,[2] a man was shot multiple times by Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents at the intersection of 26th Street and Nicollet Avenue in Minneapolis.[1][2] The man was taken to a hospital,[2] where he was pronounced dead.[2][3] An analysis by The New York Times found that several agents wrestled a man onto the sidewalk, one officer struck him with an object, and then ten shots were fired within five seconds.[1] The Department of Homeland Security stated that federal agents conducted an operation in the area; in its account, the man approached Border Patrol officers with a handgun, and an agent fired defensive shots after attempting to disarm the suspect.[2]

Victim

The individual was identified as Alex Jeffrey Pretti of south Minneapolis.[2]

Local officials stated that the victim was a 37-year-old man who lived in Minneapolis and was an American citizen.[4][2]

According to local officials, the man was a lawful gun owner and had a permit to carry a gun.[5][6] He had no serious criminal history; records searches turned up traffic tickets.[7] Other records showed he was a graduate of the University of Minnesota and held an active nurse's license.[7]

Aftermath

Following the shooting, over 100 people assembled at the site of the incident to protest.[8] News about the shooting was transmitted across rapid response networks, including Signal group chats.[9] Federal agents fired tear gas and stun grenades in clashes with protesters.[2][10] At least one person was pepper sprayed.[11] Two protesters were arrested after attempting to cross the police tape.[2][1] Protesters chanted, "I smell Nazis".[12][10] Several witnesses were transferred to the Bishop Henry Whipple Federal Building, a federal office building. Immigration and Customs Enforcement officials attempted to dismiss local police from the scene, which Minneapolis police chief Brian O'Hara refused.[2]

Investigation

Mary Moriarty, the county attorney of Hennepin County, called for the scene to be secured by local law enforcement.[13] Moriarty later stated that her office was working alongside the Minnesota Bureau of Criminal Apprehension and that she expected federal authorities to let the bureau investigate the scene.[2] Hours after the shooting, three armored vehicles from the Federal Bureau of Investigation appeared at the scene of the incident.[14] Minneapolis Police Department chief Brian O'Hara told CNN that federal officials were blocking local police from accessing the scene.[15]

According to a Trump administration official who spoke to Axios, the man had a firearm that was recovered at the scene.[16] The Department of Homeland Security provided a photograph of the alleged firearm and stated that the weapon had two magazines; according to CNN, the pictured handgun is a SIG Sauer Emperor Scorpion.[17]

Responses

Local

Minnesota governor Tim Walz (D) stated that he spoke to officials in the Trump administration after the shooting.[2][18] On social media, he called the shooting "sickening," said President Trump "must end this operation," and added "Minnesota has had it."[1] Minnesota senators Amy Klobuchar (D)âa candidate in the gubernatorial election to succeed Walz[19]âand Tina Smith (D) condemned Immigration and Customs Enforcement's continued presence following the shooting.[20]

National

President Donald Trump was briefed on the shooting.[21]

The union of US border agents blamed the shooting on politicians and the media.[22]
