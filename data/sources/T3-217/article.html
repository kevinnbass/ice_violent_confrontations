Witnesses accuse ICE of pepper spraying a minor as videos emerge of raids in Owatonna

  • By JOSH LAFOLLETTE Guest Contributor

    Josh.LaFollette

    • Author email
  • Jan 16, 2026
  • Jan 16, 2026
  • Comments
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS

Multiple videos circulating online confirm what some community members already knew and many more suspected U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents are actively conducting raids in Owatonna.

In one video posted Thursday night, multiple observers accuse an ICE agent of pepper spraying a 15-year-old girl. The video, which the uploader said was taken in the Skyline Gardens housing development, begins after the alleged incident. The girl is seated on the pavement and coughing while someone yells Why would you pepper spray a 15-year-old kid?

The circumstances leading up to the incident are not currently clear. The Peoples Press has contacted the uploader of the video for more information.


The agents in the video seemingly confirm the use of pepper spray, telling the girls mother it will take at least 45 minutes for the effects to wear off. They also offer the mother bottles of water to flush out the girls eyes. In one portion of the video, orange blotches are visible on the girls face, consistent with the stains left by pepper spray.

When one of the observers asks if an agents report will note the use of pepper spray against a minor, the agent replies, Were not going to get into the details now.

Later in the video, the girl swings her hands at an agent standing out of frame as her mother yells, You cant! An agent says he has to arrest her.

No, you dont, shes a minor, replies the mother.

The agent then says, Move her aside before I arrest her.

Best Boy raid

ICE agents also raided Best Boy Farms, formerly known as Bushel Boy, Wednesday afternoon. In an hourlong video shared on social media, agents in tactical gear can be seen taking multiple people into custody. Detainees, their hands seemingly zip-tied together, are led into unmarked vehicles bearing Iowa plates and taken from the scene. Eyewitnesses estimated five or six people were detained.

Reports of ICE raids at local workplaces have spread rapidly as the Trump administration focuses its mass deportation efforts on Minnesota. The Best Boy raid, which took place Wednesday, seems to be the first such operation to be recorded by observers.

When contacted for comment, Best Boy Farms publicly listed phone number was no longer in service.

In the video, multiple observers are stationed near the Best Boy Farms sign on 32nd Avenue NW as ICE agents conduct operations in the business parking lot. Several minutes into the recording, a masked agent approaches the group and tells them they are allowed to record, but only at a distance. While its impossible to determine the exact distance, the group appears to be standing roughly 30 yards from the parking lot. When someone in the group argues theyre already at a distance, the agent tells them its their final warning.

The conflict never escalated beyond words and gestures, but the tension between the two groups is evident in the footage. Observers are vocally critical of ICE throughout the video, heckling them with questions like, How much is your bonus?

ICEs recruitment tactics have been a source of controversy during President Donald Trumps second term. According to the Department of Homeland Security, ICEs workforce increased from 10,000 to 22,000 in just over a year, thanks in part to signing bonuses of up to $50,000.

At one point in the video, one of the agents takes out a phone and begins recording the observers as well. Others gesture at the observers, with one agent throwing up gang signs. Observers are also seen yelling at a non-uniformed man, who they allege is a member of the local Latino community that sold out Best Boy employees.

Skyline Gardens

ICEs Thursday night operation in Skyline Gardens, a manufactured home community on the north end of town, is documented in at least one other video.

In that video, recorded by an observer, a vehicle with flashing lights is seen parked behind an SUV. The doors of the SUV are hanging open. Speaking to the person recording, a woman on the scene states ICE agents removed her from her vehicle and began searching the car without her permission. According to her, the mother and daughter from the previous video approached the scene as observers, prior to the girl being pepper sprayed.

The woman who was allegedly removed from her car states she has a recording of the incident, telling an agent You cannot deny it, because I have video.

The agent replies, Im not denying it.

Several minutes in, the teenage girl can be heard screaming out of frame and the mother goes to check on her.

An agent dressed in tactical gear asks the person recording why they were parked behind agents for 15 minutes. The person behind the camera says they are observing the operation, whereas the agent accuses them of stalking.

Asked for his name and badge number, the agent only gives the observer a number. After a repeated request for his name, the agent repeats the number.

An agent wearing a Nike sweatshirt, who observers accused of pepper spraying the girl, begins speaking to the person recording in an apparent attempt to explain the situation.

As I was questioning the driver, he begins, before the sound of whistles can be heard on the recording.

Whistles are frequently used to alert people that ICE is in the area. The agent abruptly leaves mid-sentence, and the person recording states, Now theyre leaving because theres people supporting us.

Community observers

With DHS keeping local authorities in the dark about its operations, videos and eyewitness reports have become one of the main sources on information on ICE operations in Minnesota.

Some of these witnesses have undergone constitutional observer training. Constitutional observers differ from everyday observers insofar that they're officially trained volunteers who are present at law enforcement operations, demonstrations and other events to monitor for unlawful actions.

Training sessions have sprung up around the state since the recent killing of Renee Good, who was shot three times by an ICE agent in Minneapolis.

Minnesota Attorney General Keith Ellison announced a lawsuit challenging the federal governments surge into the state, and launched an online portal for people to submit documentation of federal actions impacting Minnesotans.

This form is for reporting incidents or effects related to or caused by recent federal actions in Minnesota, including but not limited to: violations of constitutional rights (racial profiling, excessive force, retaliation against protestors, observers and media), business closures, reduced healthcare access, reduced education access, other issues impacting public safety and civil liberties, federal funding cuts, federal grant terminations, terms and conditions tied to federal program participation, other administrative actions by federal agencies, the AGs website reads.
